State Hero — Karnataka

Tungabhadra

The River Queen — Mother of the Southern Fortress

Broad-shouldered and tall, with warm brown skin and kind eyes that harden to granite in battle. She wears ornate gold jewelry in the Vijayanagara style and braids jasmine flowers into her hair even before combat. Her war mace, Gajadanda, is topped with a bronze elephant head.

The Full Story

I. The Vanishing Serpents

In which the king cobras of the Western Ghats begin to disappear, and a queen turns her gaze toward the ancient forest

The report arrived at dusk, carried by a scout whose boots were caked with red laterite mud and whose face was pale beneath the copper of his skin. He knelt before Tungabhadra in the open courtyard of the Hampi garrison, where the evening light painted the ruined pillars in shades of amber, and he spoke three words that would change everything: “The serpents are vanishing.”

Tungabhadra set down the clay cup of filter coffee she had been nursing — a small ritual she maintained even as the weight of an entire kingdom pressed against her shoulders — and listened. The scout’s name was Keshava, captain of the Southern Ghats Watch, a man who had spent thirty years cataloguing the wildlife of the mountain forests with the meticulous care of a temple scribe. He was not a man given to panic. But his hands were trembling.

The king cobras of the Western Ghats were the largest venomous snakes on the subcontinent — some measured over eighteen feet, their hoods as broad as a warrior’s shield, their eyes ancient and knowing. They had inhabited these mountains since before the first human settlement, predating even the oldest songs. Keshava’s team had been tracking a particular population of forty-seven adults near the Kudremukh range. Over the past three months, thirty-one had disappeared. Not died — their bodies were never found. Not migrated — the surrounding territories showed no influx. They had simply ceased to exist in the places they had always been.

Tungabhadra rose from the stone bench, and the jasmine braided into her hair released its fragrance into the cooling air. She crossed to the wall map — a vast hand-painted chart of Karnataka’s forests that covered an entire wall of the garrison — and traced the disappearance pattern with her calloused fingertip. The locations formed a rough arc, curving inward from the forest’s edge toward a point deep in the interior where no trail existed and no survey team had ever penetrated. She studied the pattern for a long time. Then she said, quietly, “Assemble the Ghats team. We go in at first light.”

Keshava hesitated. “My lady, the interior is —”

“I know what it is,” she said. Her voice was gentle but carried the undertone of stone grinding against stone. “I grew up in a herd, Keshava. I learned to listen before I learned to speak. And the forest is trying to tell us something. We will listen.”

II. Into the Forest

In which the team descends into the primordial heart of the Western Ghats, and the forest reveals its oldest secrets

The Western Ghats at dawn were a cathedral of green. Mist hung between the trees like the breath of sleeping gods, and the canopy was so dense that the rising sun became a rumor — a vague brightening somewhere infinitely above, filtered through layer upon layer of leaves until it arrived at the forest floor as a diffuse emerald glow. The air was thick and warm, saturated with moisture, heavy with the scent of decaying wood and wild cardamom and the faint musky perfume of animals moving unseen through the undergrowth.

Tungabhadra led a team of twelve: Keshava and four of his best trackers, two herpetologists from the Mysuru Academy, a geologist named Revathi whose specialty was subterranean formations, and three of Tungabhadra’s personal guard — warriors who bore the elephant tattoo at various stages and who moved through the forest with a silence that belied their size. Tungabhadra herself carried Gajadanda strapped across her back, the bronze elephant head catching stray flickers of green-filtered light.

They followed the snake trails. A king cobra’s passage through the undergrowth leaves distinctive marks — the broad belly-scrape in soft earth, the characteristic displacement of leaf litter, the faint traces of shed venom-scales that glinted like dark glass under a tracker’s lamp. Keshava read these signs the way a scholar reads a manuscript, pausing to study a disturbed root here, a compressed patch of moss there. The trails were converging. That was the first strange thing. King cobras are solitary hunters; they do not travel in groups, do not share routes, do not cooperate. Yet here, trail after trail overlapped, braided, merged — as though the snakes were being summoned.

On the second day, they spotted their first cobra. It was enormous — over sixteen feet, its hood a deep olive-black, the pale chevron markings on its throat faded with age. It moved with a terrible deliberate grace, ignoring the humans entirely, its body flowing over roots and rocks with the fluid certainty of water finding its lowest point. They followed it for three hours, mounting motion-activated cameras on the trees as they went. The snake did not deviate. It did not hunt. It did not pause to taste the air with its tongue, which was the second strange thing, because a king cobra’s tongue is in constant motion. This one moved as though it already knew exactly where it was going.

And then, at the base of a massive strangler fig whose roots had swallowed an entire boulder like a fist closing around a stone, the cobra vanished. One moment it was there, its body a dark ribbon against the grey rock. The next, it had slipped into the earth and was gone. The team searched for twenty minutes before they found it — a fissure in the rock, barely wider than a man’s forearm, hidden behind a curtain of root-tendrils. The cameras confirmed what Keshava had suspected: every snake they had tracked had disappeared at this exact point. The same fissure. The same tree. The same impossible convergence.

Revathi the geologist pressed her ear to the rock and went very still. “There is air moving beneath us,” she said. “A lot of air. This is not a burrow. This goes deep.”

III. The Tunnel

In which the earth opens its mouth, and the scent of sandalwood rises from impossible depths

It took two days to widen the fissure enough for a human to pass through. They worked with hand tools only — Tungabhadra forbade anything mechanical, partly out of respect for the forest and partly because the vibrations of heavy equipment would scatter the snakes and destroy whatever trail they were following. The rock was basalt, ancient and dense, but the fissure followed a natural fault line that yielded to patient chiseling. Behind the rock face, they found the tunnel.

It was not a natural formation. That was clear from the first moment they shone their lamps into the darkness beyond. The walls were smooth — not water-smoothed or wind-worn, but carved. The surface bore the faint, regular marks of tools, and the cross-section was an elegant arch, perhaps seven feet tall at its apex and five feet wide, perfectly proportioned. The floor was flat and even, with a gentle downward slope that led into darkness. And from that darkness rose a current of warm air that carried the unmistakable scent of sandalwood.

The team stood at the entrance for a long time without speaking. The herpetologists were the first to voice what everyone was thinking: there should not be sandalwood this deep in the Ghats. The nearest sandalwood forests were over a hundred kilometers to the east. The warmth was wrong too — the ambient temperature inside the tunnel was at least ten degrees higher than the forest floor, which suggested geothermal activity, but the Western Ghats were not volcanically active. Nothing about this place obeyed the rules.

Keshava shone his lamp down the tunnel and they could see, on the walls, the first carvings. They were shallow, worn almost to nothing by time, but discernible: a procession of hooded serpents, their bodies intertwined in patterns that were neither purely decorative nor purely representational. They seemed to be a language of sorts, or a map, or both. Revathi ran her fingers over them and said, in a voice that was not quite steady, “These are old. Very old. I would say pre-Chalukyan, but —” She stopped. “They may be older than anything I have seen.”

Tungabhadra removed Gajadanda from her back and held it at her side. The bronze elephant head seemed to catch the lamplight and hold it, glowing with a warmth that matched the tunnel’s breath. She felt something she had not felt since childhood, since the days when she had walked with her mother’s elephant herd through monsoon storms and felt the earth tremble with messages only pachyderms could read: a vibration. Deep, rhythmic, patient. The earth was speaking. And it was saying come.

“Stay close,” she told her team. “Move slowly. Touch nothing.” And she stepped into the darkness.

IV. The Buried Temple

In which the tunnel opens into a cathedral of gold and serpents, and the team beholds what was never meant to be found

They descended for three hours. The tunnel wound through the rock in long, graceful curves — never sharp turns, never switchbacks, always that steady downward spiral, as though the builders had been mimicking the movement of a snake through grass. The carvings on the walls grew denser and more elaborate as they went deeper. Serpents gave way to scenes: figures in robes performing rituals, buildings being raised, rivers being diverted. An entire civilization was narrating itself in stone, and the deeper the team went, the older the story became.

The air grew warmer. The sandalwood scent intensified until it was almost overpowering, a rich, sweet, woody presence that seemed to coat the inside of the lungs. And then, with no warning — no widening of the tunnel, no change in the quality of the air — the walls fell away, and the team stepped into open space.

Tungabhadra raised her lamp. The light caught gold, and then more gold, and then so much gold that the reflections multiplied and shattered and filled the darkness with a thousand scattered stars. The cavern was immense — perhaps two hundred feet from floor to ceiling, and at least three hundred feet across. And standing in its center, rising from the stone floor like a prayer made physical, was a temple.

It was beautiful in a way that made the heart ache. The walls were covered in gold leaf that still gleamed despite the centuries, each sheet applied with a precision that modern craftsmen would envy. The pillars — there were dozens of them, arranged in concentric rings — were carved from a single stone each, depicting scenes from a mythology that none of the team recognized. These were not the gods of the Hindu pantheon, nor the Buddhas, nor the Jain Tirthankaras. These were something else entirely — tall, elongated figures with serene faces and bodies that seemed to blur at the edges, as though the sculptors had been depicting beings that existed only partially in physical space.

And everywhere, on every surface — the floor, the pillars, the lintels, the steps of the central sanctum — there were snakes. Hundreds of them. King cobras coiled in watchful spirals, their hoods half-raised, their tongues tasting the sudden intrusion of human scent. Rat snakes draped along the architraves like living garlands. Small, jewel-bright vine snakes wound through the carvings as though they were part of the decoration. The temple was alive with serpents, and as the team’s lamplight swept across the space, a thousand pairs of unblinking eyes turned toward them.

Three of the warriors reached for their weapons. Tungabhadra stopped them with a raised hand. She understood something the others did not — something her years with the elephants had taught her about animals that guard sacred spaces. These snakes were not hunting. They were standing watch. They had been standing watch, she realized with a chill that cut through the warm air, for a very long time.

“Lower your lamps,” she murmured. “Move slowly. They will not strike unless we threaten what they protect.” She walked forward, and the snakes parted before her like tall grass before an elephant’s tread. Behind her, the team followed in her wake, their breath shallow, their hearts hammering, surrounded on all sides by the patient, watchful silence of a thousand guardians.

V. The Artifacts

In which two relics are found at the temple's heart, and an ancient inscription tells the story of a civilization's last stand

The central sanctum was a raised platform of polished black stone, and upon it rested two objects. The first was a tablet — roughly two feet square, carved from a stone so dark it was nearly purple, with a sigil cut into its surface: a serpent devouring its own tail, but with seven heads instead of one, each head facing a different cardinal direction. When Tungabhadra approached, the tablet hummed. Not a sound exactly — more a vibration that she felt in her teeth and her sternum and the soles of her feet, a frequency that bypassed the ears entirely and spoke directly to the bones. The snakes nearest to the tablet swayed, their hoods fully extended, their bodies rigid with agitation.

The second object was a stone slab — an inscription panel, eight feet long and three feet tall, covered in dense text carved in a script that Revathi identified, with shaking voice, as a proto-Brahmi variant that predated the Ashokan edicts by at least a thousand years. It was broken in two places, and time had erased portions of the text, but enough remained to tell a story.

It took Revathi and the two herpetologists — who turned out to have training in ancient languages, because in Karnataka, scholars rarely limit themselves to one discipline — nearly six hours to produce a working translation. They sat on the stone floor of the buried temple, surrounded by snakes, their lamps arranged in a semicircle, and they pieced together the words of a civilization that had tried to erase itself from history.

The inscription told of a people who had built this temple as the repository of their greatest knowledge and most dangerous power. They called themselves the Nagavamsha — the Serpent Lineage — and they had lived in these mountains when the forests were young. Their civilization had been vast and sophisticated, spanning the entire Western Ghats from Kerala to Gujarat, and their power had been drawn from the bond between human and serpent — a bond that predated even the Tattoo Covenant by millennia.

But a monster kingdom had risen in the east. The inscription called them the Rakshasarajya — the Demon Dominion — and described them as beings of terrible hunger who consumed not just flesh but spirit, not just cities but the memory of cities, not just civilizations but the possibility that those civilizations had ever existed. They had come for the Nagavamsha’s power, and in their approach, the very earth had trembled and rivers had reversed their flow.

The Nagavamsha had fought. The inscription recorded battles of staggering scale — serpent armies clashing with shadow-creatures across mountain ranges, rivers turned to poison, forests burned to ash and regrown in a single night through desperate sorcery. But they were losing. The Rakshasarajya were too many, too hungry, too relentless. And so the Nagavamsha made their final choice: they would bury everything. The temple. The artifacts. The knowledge. Their entire civilization. They would disappear so completely that the Rakshasarajya would find nothing left to consume. And they set their serpent companions — the great king cobras, bred across generations for loyalty and longevity — to guard the buried sanctuary until the end of time, or until someone worthy came to reclaim what was hidden.

“We who were many become none. We who built towers bury them. Let the earth remember what the sky forgets. And let the serpents keep vigil where we cannot.”
— Final lines of the Nagavamsha Inscription

VI. The Monster Kingdom’s Shadow

In which the Rakshasarajya's descendants learn of the discovery, and war comes to the Western Ghats

The attack came seventeen days after the discovery. Tungabhadra had barely begun to process the implications of the buried temple — the historical rewriting it demanded, the power the serpent tablet contained, the existential questions about the Nagavamsha and their relationship to the current Naga-Manava hybrid kingdoms — when Keshava’s perimeter scouts triggered the alarm. Something was moving through the forest. Something large. Something that moved in a way that no natural creature should.

They came at night. The first sign was the silence — the complete, absolute cessation of every sound in the forest. No crickets. No frogs. No night birds. Even the wind seemed to hold its breath. Then came the smell: a thick, cloying sweetness that was the corruption of the sandalwood scent from the temple, as though someone had taken that sacred fragrance and rotted it. And then, between the trees, the shadows began to move.

The Rakshasarajya’s descendants called themselves the Chhayavanshi — the Shadow Lineage. They were not the world-eating horrors their ancestors had been; time had diminished them, as it diminishes all things. But they retained enough of the old power to be terrifying. They moved without sound and nearly without form — shapes that were almost human but slightly wrong, proportions that shifted when observed directly, faces that were holes in the darkness rather than features within it. They had been searching for the Nagavamsha temple for three thousand years, following rumors and fragments of myth across the length of the subcontinent, and now they had finally traced its location. How they had learned of Tungabhadra’s discovery, she never determined. Perhaps they had spies among the forest officials. Perhaps the tablet’s activation had sent out a signal that only shadow-kind could hear.

The battle of the Western Ghats was fought on two fronts. Above ground, Tungabhadra’s warriors engaged the Chhayavanshi in the thick forest, where visibility was measured in feet and the enemy could emerge from any shadow. Tungabhadra fought with Gajadanda in both hands, the bronze elephant head singing as it cut through darkness that tried to reform around each blow. She fought the way she had been taught by the elephants — not with speed or agility, but with devastating, unstoppable momentum. Where she charged, the shadows broke. Where she stood, the ground held. Her warriors rallied around her like calves around a matriarch, taking courage from her solidity, her refusal to yield a single step.

Below ground, a smaller force attempted to breach the tunnel. Keshava led the defense there, and it was the snakes who turned the tide. The king cobras, roused from their ancient vigil by the intrusion of shadow-kind, attacked with a fury that shocked even Tungabhadra’s seasoned warriors. The great serpents struck at the Chhayavanshi with a precision that went beyond instinct — they targeted the points where the shadow-forms were most solid, where the darkness was most concentrated, and their venom, it turned out, burned shadow-flesh as easily as it burned human tissue. Perhaps more easily. The tunnel became a killing ground, and the snakes showed no mercy.

The battle lasted two nights and a day. When it ended, the Chhayavanshi had retreated, but they had not been destroyed. Tungabhadra, bleeding from a dozen wounds and leaning on Gajadanda for support, watched the last of the shadows melt back into the forest and knew, with the certainty of one who reads the earth, that they would return.

VII. The Decision

In which Tungabhadra chooses to bury what was buried, and entrusts an eternal vigil to new guardians

The council of war convened on the floor of the buried temple itself, because there was nowhere else secure enough to hold it. Tungabhadra sat on the steps of the central sanctum, the serpent tablet behind her still humming its low, bone-deep note, and she listened as her advisors argued.

Revathi wanted to bring in researchers. The inscription alone, she said, would rewrite the history of the Indian subcontinent. The Nagavamsha civilization was unknown to modern scholarship — its discovery would be the archaeological event of the millennium. The serpent tablet’s power, if understood and harnessed, could be a weapon against the Void Maw. How could they justify burying it again when the universe itself was at stake?

The herpetologists agreed with Revathi. Keshava did not. He had fought beside the snakes in the tunnel and he had seen what the Chhayavanshi were. “The Nagavamsha buried this place for a reason,” he said. His voice was raw from smoke and exertion, and there were new grey streaks in his beard that had not been there three weeks ago. “They did not bury it because they were weak. They buried it because the alternative was worse. If the Chhayavanshi take the tablet, we lose more than a battle. We lose the memory of everything these people were.”

Tungabhadra listened until every voice had spoken and every argument had been made. The snakes coiled quietly around her, and she felt their patience, their ancient willingness to wait. She thought of her mother, who had died defending Karnataka and who had taught her, in the few years they had together, that true strength was not the ability to take but the ability to protect. She thought of the Nagavamsha, who had loved their civilization enough to destroy it rather than see it devoured. She thought of the Void Maw, somewhere beyond the stars, consuming everything in its path.

“Seal the tunnel,” she said.

The silence that followed was absolute. Even the serpents seemed to hold still.

“Seal it,” she repeated. “Collapse the entrance. Fill it with rubble. Plant new trees over the site. The Nagavamsha chose to disappear, and we will honor that choice. The tablet’s power is real, but power that must be hidden to be safe is not power we are ready to wield.” She looked at Revathi. “Copy the inscription. Every word, every symbol. That knowledge, at least, we can preserve. But the tablet stays in the earth. And we will set our own guard.”

She chose twelve warriors from her personal guard — the most loyal, the most disciplined, the most skilled at patience. She stationed them in the forest around the sealed entrance, dressed as wildlife rangers, equipped with tracking gear and communication devices, indistinguishable from the legitimate conservation teams that patrolled the Western Ghats. Their true mission was known only to Tungabhadra and Keshava: watch the forest, watch the shadows, and if the Chhayavanshi return, die before the tunnel is breached again.

The snakes, unbidden, resumed their vigil. Within weeks of the tunnel’s sealing, the king cobras returned to the area in numbers that made the herpetologists weep with confused joy. They coiled over the collapsed entrance as though the rubble were their ancestral home. They nested in the roots of the strangler fig. They patrolled the surrounding forest in slow, deliberate circuits, tasting the air for shadow, for threat, for the corrupted sweetness that heralded the Chhayavanshi’s approach. Three thousand years of guardianship had not ended. It had merely acquired reinforcements.

VIII. Legacy

In which the weight of hidden knowledge reshapes a queen, and the world changes beneath the surface of the known

The discovery of the buried temple changed Tungabhadra in ways that were visible to those who knew her well and invisible to everyone else. She carried herself differently now — not with less confidence, but with a gravity that went beyond the physical. She had always been the most beloved of the southern heroes, the one who led through warmth and trust. Now there was something behind her eyes that her warriors could not quite name: the look of someone who has glimpsed the depth of history and understood that the present is a thin skin stretched over an abyss of forgotten things.

She told no one outside her inner circle about the temple’s true contents. At the Grand Council, she mentioned the expedition in passing — a routine investigation of wildlife patterns in the Western Ghats, nothing more. But she began to ask questions that unsettled the other Regional Leaders. She inquired about the Naga-Manava hybrid kingdom — were they truly the descendants of ancient serpent-worshippers, or could they be the actual descendants of the Nagavamsha themselves? She studied the accounts of the Void Maw with a new intensity, looking for patterns that matched the inscription’s description of the Rakshasarajya — entities that consumed not just matter but memory, not just worlds but the possibility of worlds.

The parallels haunted her. The Rakshasarajya, as described in the Nagavamsha inscription, sounded disturbingly like a local manifestation of whatever force the Void Maw represented. If the Nagavamsha had fought a smaller version of this cosmic hunger three thousand years ago, then the buried temple was not just an archaeological curiosity — it was evidence. Evidence that the Void Maw, or something like it, had touched the Earth before. And the Nagavamsha had survived. Not by fighting, not by running, but by hiding so completely that the hunger could find nothing left to consume.

Was that the answer? Not a weapon, but a disappearance? Could the entire planet learn to bury itself the way the Nagavamsha had buried their temple?

Tungabhadra did not share this thought with the Grand Council. It was too radical, too terrifying, too close to surrender to be spoken aloud in a chamber full of warriors who defined themselves by their willingness to fight. But she kept it. She turned it over in her mind the way she had once turned river stones in her hands as a child, feeling for their shape, their weight, their hidden contours. And she returned, in secret, to the copy of the Nagavamsha inscription, searching its damaged text for the one thing she had not yet found: instructions. Because a civilization that chose to bury itself must have left instructions for the unburying, and those instructions, if they existed, might be the key to everything.

Meanwhile, the Chhayavanshi did not rest. Reports filtered in from Keshava’s disguised sentinels: shadows at the forest’s edge, testing the defenses, probing for weakness. Twice they attempted to breach the perimeter and were driven back — once by the warriors, once by the snakes alone. They were patient, these shadow-children. They had waited three thousand years. They could wait longer. But so could the serpents. And so, now, could Tungabhadra.

Beneath the Western Ghats, in a cavern of gold and darkness, the buried temple waited. The serpent tablet hummed its low, eternal note. The carvings told their story to no one. And the snakes coiled and uncoiled in their slow, ancient rhythm, guarding what had been hidden, what had been forgotten, what might yet save the world — or end it.

Connection to the MahaHulmane Saga

The Rakshasarajya & the Void Maw

The Nagavamsha inscription describes the Rakshasarajya as beings that consumed “not just flesh but spirit, not just cities but the memory of cities.” This language mirrors almost exactly the accounts given by the alien Void Walkers about the Void Maw. If the Rakshasarajya were an earthly manifestation of the same cosmic hunger, then the buried temple may contain the only historical record of a civilization that survived contact with this force — not through combat, but through erasure.

The Serpent Tablet’s Power

The seven-headed serpent sigil on the buried tablet hums at a frequency that resonates with the bones of anyone who approaches it. If this artifact is a weapon — or a shield — forged by a civilization that understood the Void Maw’s nature, it could be the key to the Grand Council’s war. But Tungabhadra sealed it away, believing humanity is not ready. The question that haunts her: will humanity be ready before the Void Maw arrives?

The Naga-Manava Connection

The Naga-Manava hybrid kingdom — half-serpent, half-human beings who sit on the Grand Council’s outer ring — may be the direct descendants of the Nagavamsha. If so, they hold ancestral knowledge about the buried temple and the Chhayavanshi that could prove invaluable. But the Naga-Manava have their own agenda, and their relationship with human civilization has always been one of guarded secrecy.

The Strategy of Erasure

Tungabhadra’s darkest thought: the Nagavamsha survived by making themselves nothing. Could Earth do the same? Could the Grand Council, instead of fighting the Void Maw, choose to hide — to bury everything, as the Nagavamsha did, and wait for the hunger to pass? It is a strategy of desperation, but it is the only strategy that has ever worked. And the instructions, if they exist, lie somewhere in the damaged text of the inscription that Revathi copied by lamplight in a temple of gold and serpents.
