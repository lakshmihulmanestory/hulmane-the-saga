From the Age of Animals to the Final Stand

This is the story of how twenty-eight kingdoms, six regions, seven continents, five alien civilizations, and five ancient hybrid bloodlines came together to face the greatest threat the universe has ever known. It begins, as all great stories do, with a bond between human and beast.

Chapter VII
The MahaHulmane

Before the kingdoms had names, before the borders were drawn in blood and treaty, there was only the land and its creatures. The great subcontinent of Bharata teemed with life — the Asiatic lions of the western grasslands, the one-horned rhinoceros of the eastern marshes, the snow leopards that haunted the northern peaks, the elephants that moved like living mountains through the southern forests.

The first humans who settled these lands did not conquer the animals. They learned from them. The fishermen of the Sundarbans studied the fishing cat's patience. The hill tribes of Meghalaya followed the clouded leopard through paths that no human eye could find. The desert clans of Rajasthan survived only because they moved like the chinkara — swift, silent, and always sensing danger before it arrived.

Over centuries, something extraordinary happened. The bond between human and beast deepened beyond mere imitation. Shamans spoke of a “spirit bridge” — an invisible connection between the people of a region and its dominant animal. When the lion of Gujarat roared, the people of that land felt courage surge in their blood. When the elephant of Karnataka mourned its dead, the people wept without knowing why.

This was not magic. It was something older and deeper — a covenant written in the shared DNA of land, animal, and human. The animals were not pets or symbols. They were the other half of a people's soul. And as the centuries turned, this bond would manifest in ways no one could have predicted.

“We did not tame the beasts. We grew together. Like roots of two trees intertwined beneath the earth.”
— From the Oral Histories of the First Peoples

The legend says it happened on the night of a blood moon, roughly 2,500 years ago. A child was born in what would later be called Gujarat — and on its right shoulder, glowing faintly in the darkness of the birth-hut, was the perfect imprint of a lion's paw.

The elders were terrified. The shamans were ecstatic. They had spoken of this for generations — the prophecy of the “Marked Ones.” The spirit bridge, they said, had finally become visible. The bond between human and beast was no longer invisible. It had written itself on human skin.

Within a single generation, children across the entire subcontinent began to be born with marks. In Assam, the rhinoceros footprint. In Kerala, the elephant's broad pad. In Sikkim, the delicate print of the red panda. Every region, every clan, every settlement — their children bore the mark of the dominant animal of their land.

But it was the warrior-sage Dakshinamurthi who discovered the greater truth: the marks were alive. When a young marked warrior performed an act of extraordinary courage, their footprint expanded. It grew upward — from a paw print to a leg, from a leg to a torso, and in the rarest cases, to the full body of the beast. The tattoo was not decoration. It was a measure of power, a living record of achievement, and a direct channel to the spirit of the sacred animal.

Dakshinamurthi codified the system into four stages and called it the Chihnachakra — the Wheel of Marks. The Tattoo Covenant was born. Every kingdom in Bharata adopted it. And from that day forward, the path to leadership was not determined by birth or wealth, but by the growth of one's mark.

“The beast does not choose the worthy. It reveals them.”
— Dakshinamurthi, Codifier of the Tattoo Covenant

The Tattoo Covenant transformed Bharata. Gone were the old aristocracies of blood. In their place rose a meritocracy of marks. A farmer's son with a full-body elephant tattoo outranked a king's heir with merely a footprint. Power became earned, visible, undeniable.

Over the next five centuries, twenty-eight distinct kingdoms crystallized — each centered around a specific sacred beast. The boundaries were not drawn by generals but by the animals themselves. Where the Asiatic lion roamed, that was Gujarat. Where the one-horned rhino grazed, that was Assam. The animals defined the land, and the land defined its people.

Each kingdom developed its own trials — the tests through which warriors could advance their tattoos. In Tamil Nadu, the Nilgiri Tahr demanded that aspirants scale the sheer cliffs of the Western Ghats blindfolded, trusting the mountain goat's instinct. In West Bengal, the Fishing Cat trials required warriors to survive a month in the mangrove swamps with nothing but their wits. In Rajasthan, the Chinkara test meant crossing the Thar Desert alone during the hottest month.

The current generation of twenty-eight State Heroes represents the finest warriors their kingdoms have produced. Some are noble beyond measure — like Tungabhadra of Karnataka, whose gentle strength mirrors her elephant companion. Others are terrifying — like Vajramukha of Bihar, the Diamond-Faced, whose gaur-like rage has left a trail of broken challengers across the eastern plains.

And then there are the morally complex ones. Simhavikrama of Gujarat bears the lion's full mark but rules with an iron fist, believing that only absolute strength can protect his people. Mrigatrishna of Rajasthan — the Desert Mirage — earned her full mark through deception and cunning rather than combat, proving that the tattoo rewards effectiveness, not virtue.

“Twenty-eight beasts. Twenty-eight champions. Twenty-eight different definitions of strength.”
— Regional Historian Scroll, Year of the Convergence

Peace, it turned out, was not the natural state of twenty-eight powerful kingdoms. The Tattoo Covenant had created heroes — but heroes, by their nature, sought glory. And glory, when twenty-eight champions all seek it simultaneously, inevitably leads to war.

The first regional conflicts erupted between neighboring kingdoms whose animals shared territory. Gujarat and Rajasthan — lion and gazelle — clashed over the western borderlands. The elephant kingdoms of the south (Karnataka, Kerala, and Jharkhand) formed and broke alliances in a constantly shifting dance of power. In the northeast, the eight small but fierce kingdoms fought a guerrilla war that lasted three generations.

It was during the chaos of the Regional Wars that the concept of Combination Tattoos emerged. When a warrior so thoroughly dominated their region that every other State Hero submitted, something unprecedented happened — the tattoos of the defeated merged into the victor's mark. A Regional Leader was born, bearing a chimeric beast that combined elements of every animal in their domain.

Six regions stabilized: South, West, East, North, Northeast, and Central. Each led by a warrior bearing the terrible burden of the Combination Tattoo — the voices of multiple beast-spirits whispering, arguing, and roaring in their mind. Many who achieved this power were driven to madness. Those who survived became legends.

The wars consumed untold lives. The Battle of the Five Elephants alone — when Karnataka, Kerala, and Jharkhand's elephant-bonded warriors met on the Deccan Plateau — reshaped the landscape itself. Trees were uprooted in swathes. Rivers changed course. The ground still trembles in that region on the anniversary, they say.

“We fought each other with the fury of beasts. We did not know that something far worse was watching us, measuring our strength, and deciding whether we were worth consuming.”
— Panchanada of Punjab, Reflections After the Northern Campaigns

The astronomers of Ujjain were the first to notice. Stars in the constellation of Sagittarius were disappearing. Not dimming — disappearing. One night a star would be there, ancient and reliable as a grandfather's heartbeat. The next night, blackness. As if something had swallowed it whole.

At first, only the stargazers worried. But then the gravitational anomalies began. Tides surged without cause. Birds migrated out of season. The animals — the sacred beasts themselves — grew restless. The lions of Gujarat paced and roared at the night sky. The elephants of the south formed circles, their trunks pointing upward, trumpeting warnings that no one could translate.

It was the alien civilizations who confirmed the worst. They had been watching Earth from a distance — some for centuries, others for millennia. When their emissary, Xel'Vithara of the Void Walkers, materialized before the Regional Leaders of Bharata, the message was brief and devastating: “The Void Maw is coming. It has consumed twelve galaxies. Yours is next. You have perhaps twenty years.”

The Void Maw. An entity so ancient it predated the formation of most stars. Not a creature, exactly — more like a wound in spacetime that hungered. It moved through the cosmos consuming everything — matter, energy, even light itself. In its wake, nothing remained. Not darkness. Not void. Not even the absence of something. Simply... nothing.

The regional wars stopped overnight. For the first time in a thousand years, the six Regional Leaders of Bharata sat at the same table. The hybrid kingdoms — the Naga-Manava, the Garuda-Kin, the Vyaghra-Nara — emerged from hiding. Ambassadors from every continent on Earth received the same message. And slowly, reluctantly, fearfully, the greatest alliance in the history of all worlds began to take shape.

“I have seen it consume a binary star system in seventeen minutes. Your sun would take three.”
— Xel'Vithara, Emissary of the Void Walkers

The Grand Chamber was built in neutral territory — a massive circular arena carved into the bedrock of the Vindhya Mountains, where no kingdom held claim. It took a year to construct and was designed to hold the most powerful beings on Earth, and beyond.

The inner ring seated Bharata's six Regional Leaders. They came armored in their Combination Tattoos, the chimeric beasts writhing on their skin as if alive. The tension was immediate — these warriors had been killing each other's people for centuries. Old grudges do not die because a new threat arrives. They simmer.

The second ring held the World Representatives — seven warriors from the seven continents. Ashokamara of Africa, whose bond with the African elephant dwarfed even the Indian elephant champions in raw power. Fenrisulfr of Europe, the wolf-bonded berserker whose presence made the room temperature drop. Tlaloc-Quetzal of South America, who spoke to jaguars and moved like rain through the jungle.

The third ring belonged to the Space Kingdoms. Five alien civilizations, each more incomprehensible than the last. The Void Walkers, who existed partially outside of time. The Crystalline Hegemony, beings of living mineral who communicated through harmonic vibrations. The Primal Swarm, a collective intelligence of billions of insectoid organisms that spoke with a single voice.

And in the outer ring, the most unsettling presence of all — the Hybrid Kingdoms. The Naga-Manava, half-serpent, half-human, who had lived beneath India's rivers since before recorded history. The Garuda-Kin, who could soar above the clouds and see the Void Maw's approach with their own eyes. They had been hiding among humanity for millennia, and now, for the first time, they sat openly.

At the center of it all: an empty throne. The seat of the MahaHulmane — the Grand Champion who would lead this impossible alliance into battle. The title would not be given. It would be earned. And every being in that chamber wanted it.

“Look around you. We are not allies. We are not friends. We are prey that has learned to bare its teeth. That will have to be enough.”
— Dandakarni of Chhattisgarh, Opening Address to the Grand Council

The Grand Council is in session. The empty throne at its center burns with an eternal golden flame, waiting for the one who will sit upon it. But no champion has yet emerged who commands the respect of all four rings — India, World, Space, and Hybrid.

Meanwhile, the twenty-eight State Heroes of Bharata train like never before. The regional rivalries have not ended — they have been channeled into the competition for the MahaHulmane throne. Each hero knows that whoever leads this alliance will have the power to reshape the world after the Void Maw is defeated. If it is defeated.

The Void Maw grows closer. Astronomers report that the blackout zone has reached the outer edges of the Milky Way. They have perhaps twelve years before it arrives. Twelve years to turn an alliance of bitter enemies into a fighting force capable of challenging a cosmic god.

Alliances form and fracture daily. Simhavikrama of Gujarat courts the African representative, seeing a kindred spirit in another beast-king. Himavati of Himachal Pradesh has forged an unlikely bond with the Crystalline Hegemony, whose mineral forms remind her of her mountain home. Sundarvani of West Bengal, the Fishing Cat's champion, has become the de facto diplomat — her quiet patience and ability to read people makes her invaluable in negotiations.

But dark currents flow beneath the surface. Some council members secretly believe the Void Maw cannot be stopped and are plotting escape routes — ways to save their own people at the expense of others. The Naga-Manava speak of retreating to the Earth's core. The Void Walkers hint that they can phase out of this dimension entirely. And within Bharata itself, there are whispers of a faction that wants to communicate with the Void Maw — to offer it Earth in exchange for their own survival.

The MahaHulmane is not just a title. It is a desperate hope. It is the belief that somewhere, among all these warriors and monsters and aliens and gods, there exists one being who can unite them all. One being whose strength, wisdom, or sheer force of will can turn this fractured council into a weapon capable of facing the abyss.

The throne waits. The sky darkens. And somewhere, a tattoo is beginning to glow.
